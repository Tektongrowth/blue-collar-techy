## FULL SCRIPT

### HOOK (0:00 – 0:30)

[B-ROLL: jobsite footage — hands laying pavers, concrete being poured, truck loaded with stone]

I spent ten years running a hardscape crew. Stone, wood, steel. Six-figure design-build jobs. And then I walked away from it and started a tech company.

People ask me how that happens.

So here's the whole story. The jobsite days. The reason I quit. And why I think most of the marketing advice contractors get right now is garbage.

[B-ROLL: talking head close-up, plain tee, desk behind him]

---

### CHAPTER 1: The Blue-Collar Roots (0:30 – 2:00)

[B-ROLL: old family photos, generic plumber's truck footage, wildland fire footage]

I grew up blue collar.

My dad ran a plumbing company. I grew up working for him. And I was doing side jobs before I was old enough to drive. Raking leaves. Small stuff. Whatever put money in my pocket.

Out of high school I got into excavation for a while. Then I spent a few years with the Forest Service as a wildland firefighter.

That kind of work shapes you. You learn to show up. You learn to work tired. You learn pretty quick that excuses don't move the job forward. And honestly, that's still a big part of how I look at business today.

So when I talk to contractors now, I'm not pretending I'm someone who's been in a classroom his whole life. I know what the grind feels like. I lived it.

[PATTERN INTERRUPT] And then in my twenties, everything shifted.

---

### CHAPTER 2: The Build (2:00 – 4:30)

[B-ROLL: photos of early hardscape projects, finished patios, crew working]

I got a job with a high-end landscape maintenance company. Mowing lawns, pulling weeds, keeping properties clean.

But every now and then we'd do a project. A flagstone walk. A paver patio. A stone staircase. And that's the work I actually cared about.

So I did what a lot of guys do. I bought a truck and a lawnmower, and I started my own thing.

[B-ROLL: truck loaded with mower, early solo work]

It started as Nicholas Landscaping. Maintenance paid the bills. Projects were the goal. And over a few years it grew into Tekton Design Build. Custom outdoor living. Six-figure jobs. Stone, wood, and steel.

The name Tekton mattered to me. It's a biblical word. It was used for craftsmen, the guys who built the Old Testament temples. Technicians of their craft. That felt like who we were.

[B-ROLL: close-up shots of finished stonework, craftsmanship details]

A lot of those years were good. A lot of them were brutal too.

I'll tell you one story. We had seven yards of concrete coming on a morning for a curb and a set of stairs. Big job. And I show up, and one of my guys is passed out. Another guy just no-showed.

So it was me and one other dude, trying to pour seven yards of concrete and build this thing before it beat us.

We pulled it off, mostly because the sky stayed cloudy and the concrete didn't set too fast. That's the kind of day that burns in.

[B-ROLL: concrete work, curbing]

A couple years later, I had back surgery. I was out for a few months. And the team I'd built kept the company running. No drama. No panic. They just handled it. And that was one of the first times I looked at what we'd built and thought, alright, this is real.

---

### CHAPTER 3: The Turn (4:30 – 6:30)

[B-ROLL: talking head close-up]

But here's the thing.

Even with all that, the business wasn't giving me the life I started it for.

I had three kids. A bad back. A company that depended on a lot from me. And I had to be honest with myself. The path I was on was not taking me where I wanted to go.

And the truth is, the part of the business I loved most wasn't pouring concrete anymore. It was marketing it.

I liked figuring out why the phone rang. I liked working on the website. Building the brand. Messing with the sales process. Systems. Automations. Stuff that had nothing to do with a shovel.

[B-ROLL: desk, computer, early marketing work]

So I leaned into it.

And I'll be honest, I paid a lot of tuition in mistakes along the way.

When I ran my hardscape company, I hired marketing agencies. And most of them left a lot to be desired. Big promises. Weak follow-through. Fancy words around average work. I'd write checks thinking, great, that's handled. It wasn't.

So I started teaching myself.

---

### CHAPTER 4: Going Full Tech (6:30 – 8:30)

[B-ROLL: screen recording of Google Business Profile, CRM dashboards, AI tool interfaces]

SEO. Websites. CRMs. Automations. AI. I got in there because I had to. Then I got good at it because I cared. And then I started helping other contractors because I realized a lot of them were dealing with the same mess I had been.

[B-ROLL: talking head]

That's where Tekton Growth came from. Same builder mindset. Different trade.

I'm not some marketing guy who wandered into the contractor world. I'm a contractor who figured out the marketing world because nobody was doing it right for us.

[B-ROLL: jobsite split with desk view — shows both worlds]

And here's something I think about a lot. I saw a better future in the digital world than in the dirt world. But I chose to work with dirt-world people. On purpose. Because that's still my people.

[PATTERN INTERRUPT] Which brings me to the part I actually want to talk about.

---

### CHAPTER 5: The Hot Take + Why Blue Collar Techy Exists (8:30 – 10:00)

[B-ROLL: talking head, direct to camera, serious expression]

Your marketing agency is not the hero of your business. You are.

I learned that the hard way. Good marketing matters. Good SEO matters. Good systems matter. But no agency can fix a weak reputation, slow follow-up, a bad sales process, or a business that doesn't do what it says it does.

The owners who understand that usually grow.

The ones who think writing a check solves the whole problem usually get burned. Every single time.

[B-ROLL: logo animation — Blue Collar Techy mark]

And that's why I built Blue Collar Techy.

Because most advice for contractors misses the mark. A lot of it comes from people who have never been in the seat. Or it comes from people talking about AI and SEO and automation like every owner has forty hours a week to become a full-time marketer.

That misses too.

Blue Collar Techy sits in the middle of those two worlds. I know the blue-collar side. I've spent the last several years deep in the tech side. And this is where I put the straight answer. What's worth paying attention to. What's hype. What gets calls. What saves time. What's a waste of money.

---

[B-ROLL: quick montage — jobsite, desk, YouTube cover, newsletter]

So if you're a contractor, a trades owner, a guy running a crew and a business, and you want practical stuff on AI and marketing and tech without the guru nonsense, you're in the right place.

[CTA]

Hit subscribe. I'm putting out videos every week. No fluff. No hype. Just stuff you can actually use.

And if you want to go deeper, the next video I'd watch is the one right there. I break down the three marketing moves every contractor should be doing before they spend a dollar on ads.

I'll see you in the next one.
