Reviews 2026

How to get more Google reviews as a contractor (without sounding desperate).

Most contractors ask once, at the wrong moment, and never follow up. The contractors getting reviews consistently are asking at three specific moments, saying something simple, and tracking who followed through. Here is a simple way to handle it in 2026, with the new Google rules in mind.

BY NICK CONLEY FOUNDER · BLUE COLLAR TECHY
// Why this year is different

Getting more Google reviews used to be a numbers game. Ask everybody, take every star, move on. That game got rewritten in March 2026.

Google rolled out a major review policy update this spring. Deletion rates across business profiles jumped more than 600% between early 2025 and mid-2026. Home services and contractor profiles took the biggest hit, with a deletion rate running 2.8 to 3.4 percent according to the monitoring that tracks this stuff.

A few things in the update matter to you. Incentivized reviews are now explicitly against policy. Discounts, prizes, gift cards, and loyalty points all count. Google's enforcement is retroactive, so reviews you collected years ago using the old "leave a review and get $25 off" playbook can now get wiped, and the profile can get dinged in rankings.

Reviews from current or former employees, subcontractors you hire regularly, or family members are also flagged. Google's AI system is getting much better at spotting coordinated patterns and suspicious reviewer accounts, especially in the contractor space where review manipulation has been a known problem for years.

The short version: you still need reviews. You need more of them than you have. But the way you ask, who you ask, and what you offer in exchange has to change.

Average top-ranking contractor profile has about 47 Google reviews. For plumbing it's 215. For pest control it's 265. 59% of customers only trust a star rating once a business is past 20 reviews. If you're under 20, your rating doesn't carry weight yet.

// The core principle

Ask three times, not once.

Most contractors ask for a review once. Usually in person, at the end of the job, when the customer says "thanks, looks great." The owner says "hey if you have a minute a Google review would really help." Then they leave and nothing happens.

That one-shot ask usually doesn't convert very well. It's not that customers are lazy. It's that life happens. Kids, dinner, work, the next thing.

The contractors who consistently add 5 to 10 new reviews a month are asking at three separate moments, and each ask uses a different channel. It doesn't come off like nagging when each ask is spaced out and sent a different way. Each one lives in a different place in the customer's head.

Think of it like base prep before you pour a patio. You compact the stone once, you let it settle, then you compact again. One pass doesn't lock it in. The customer's intent to leave a review works the same way.

// The three ask moments
Moment 01 In person · Job day

The walkthrough ask.

When you finish the final walkthrough and the customer is happy, that is the emotional peak. You ask in person, face to face, and keep it short. No QR codes, no tablets, no printed cards. Just the ask.

"Would you be willing to leave us a Google review? It's the single thing
that helps me the most. I'll send you the link by text tonight so you
don't have to search for it. Totally fine if not."

That last line matters. Giving them the out keeps it from feeling like pressure, and it actually lifts follow-through rates because the customer feels in control.

What you're doing

Priming the ask. You are not collecting the review here. You are setting up the text that comes next so it doesn't feel cold.

Moment 02 Text · Same evening

The same-day text.

Send a text the same evening, before the customer goes to sleep. Personal, from your phone or a number they recognize. Not a marketing blast. Keep it under two sentences with a direct link to your review URL.

"Hey [name] this is [your name] with [company]. Thanks again for
having us out today. If you have a second, here's the link for that
Google review we talked about: [short URL]. Appreciate you."

Use a short link. Get your Google review link from your profile, drop it into a shortener if you want, and save it in your phone so it's one copy-paste. If the link looks like a giant string of characters, fewer people tap it.

What you're doing

Catching them while the job is still fresh. Most reviews that land come from this text, not from the in-person ask or the follow-up email.

Moment 03 Email · Day 3

The three-day follow-up.

Three days after the job wraps, send one short email. This is the safety net for customers who meant to leave the review but got busy. This one converts a surprising amount of the people who didn't respond to the text.

Subject: One quick favor

Hey [name],

Hope the [patio / roof / system] is working well for you. No pressure,
but if you have 60 seconds, a Google review would mean a lot:
[link]

Thanks,
[your name]

That's it. No attachments, no header image, no company graphic. Plain text looks like it came from you personally because it did. People are more likely to respond when it feels like it came from you.

What you're doing

Catching the people who had the best intentions and life got in the way. This is often the most useful of the three asks, and a lot of contractors skip it.

The contractors getting reviews consistently aren't asking harder. They're asking three times in three different places.
Rule of three asks
// Track who followed through

Most contractors never check who actually left a review.

This is the piece that separates the shops that get reviews consistently from the ones that don't. A simple tracking sheet with four columns: customer name, job date, review requested (yes/no at each of the three moments), review landed (yes/no). That's it.

Once you have that sheet, two things happen. One, you stop double-asking customers who already left a review. Two, the people who never left one become a list you can touch once more, thirty days out, with a lighter ask.

Run this in a Google Sheet, in your CRM, in a paper notebook if that's what you'll actually use. The format doesn't matter. The habit does. Most shops have a 100-job backlog of customers they could have asked but never tracked, and every one of those was a review left on the table.

// The new Google rules

What not to do in 2026.

Since the March 2026 update, a handful of tactics will either get reviews deleted, get the profile suppressed, or both. Skip all of them even if they used to work.

  • No incentives. Do not offer a discount, gift card, raffle entry, tip, or anything of value for a review. Google's AI is now flagging these patterns and the enforcement is retroactive.
  • No employee or subcontractor reviews. Not your crew, not the guys you sub to regularly, not office staff. Google is cross-referencing reviewer accounts with business profiles.
  • No family reviews. Your brother-in-law leaving a five-star takes the review down and flags the profile.
  • No bulk third-party services. Services that claim they can "get you 20 reviews in a week" from their network got wiped out in the update and your profile can get penalized alongside them.
  • No AI-generated review content. Don't hand customers prewritten AI review copy to paste. Google detects the content itself.
  • No review gating. The practice of asking "would you give us a 5-star?" and only sending happy ones to Google is explicitly banned and easy for Google to detect.

The takeaway is simple. Google wants reviews from real customers writing real words about real work. Everything that feels like a shortcut is coming off the board.

// Handle the 4-star problem

Reply to every review. Especially the 4-star ones.

Most contractors respond to 5-star reviews with a "thanks!" and leave 4-star and below unanswered. That reads wrong to future customers. The 4-star reviews are the ones potential customers actually read carefully to see if the business is honest.

A good reply to a 4-star review names the specific thing the customer called out, owns the part you could have done better, and closes with what they can expect next time. Two or three sentences. Not defensive, not groveling.

A bad 4-star review reply sounds like "We're sorry you didn't have a 5-star experience, please reach out privately." That sounds canned. Specific beats polished. Every. Single. Time.

Reply rate is visible too. Strong contractor profiles reply to 100 percent of reviews. If yours doesn't, that's an easy thing to clean up.

// How many do you need

Your target is your top competitor, not some magic number.

There is no single review count that guarantees a map pack spot. What matters is where you sit relative to the top three in your category in your city.

Pull up the map pack for your main service and your city right now. Count the reviews on each of the three listings. If the top one has 180 and you have 40, your target is 180, not some random goal of "100 by year end."

Benchmarks from the home services data in 2026 look like this. Median rating is 4.84 stars. To compete on rating alone you want a 4.8 to 4.9. Volume varies wildly by trade. Plumbing top listings average 215. Pest control 265. Hardscape and remodeling tend to sit lower, often 50 to 120. Check your own trade in your own city. That's the only number that matters for you.

If you're at 12 reviews and the top contractor in town has 200, you're probably not closing that gap in a month. Set a goal of 5 to 8 new reviews per month and treat it like billable work. Do it every month and it adds up.
